# Product Vision

ABRIR is a game about institutions that insist impossible spaces are manageable because they have forms for them.

The Instituto Travessia sends teams through generated passages into rooms described as simulations, corrupted servers, or nonbinding realities. Objects recovered inside remain physically real and economically useful. The Institute keeps up to fifteen percent of every haul, then may sell those same objects back at market price. Across the passage network, A Chave Geral sells unauthorized access, reroutes rooms, and turns traversal into an illicit economy.

## Player fantasy

The player is not merely clearing dungeons. They are entering a machine that produces jurisdictional uncertainty. Every run asks three questions:

1. What can be recovered?
2. How long can the team remain before another state interlaces with this one?
3. Who gets to declare the recovered world real afterward?

## Design pillars

### The map is evidence

A run's map is generated, serialized, inspectable, and reproducible. The same state can be replayed, shared, audited, modified, or used as the source for a story event.

### Partnership is the character system

The field unit is a pair, not a lone avatar. Switching should feel tactical and personal: different ranges, health pools, recovery patterns, movement rhythms, and reactions to the same room.

### Risk changes the space

Remaining after the safe window does not merely increase enemy health. It introduces another generated state into the current one. Routes overlap, rooms become accessible, enemy groups cross boundaries, and rare objects become possible.

### Loot is narrative material

Recovered objects are not anonymous color tiers. Their names, provenance, behavior, legal status, and market value make each haul part of the world's argument.

### Lusophone locations shape systems

Brazil is the main center, with later routes across Portugal, Angola, Mozambique, Cape Verde, Guinea-Bissau, São Tomé and Príncipe, Equatorial Guinea, and Timor-Leste. Locations should change architecture, language texture, room logic, institutions, music, and object pools—not simply reskin the walls.
